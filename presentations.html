Karim, Shuan O. 09/25/2021. A diachrony of Kurdish imperfective affixes. To be presented at the 5th International Conference on Kurdish Linguistics (ICKL5) at the University of Graz.

Abstract

There are many systems for marking the imperfective, including the present tense (the implicit realm of the imperfective), aspect in Northern, Central, and Southern Kurdish varieties. The most common strategies for marking the imperfective are prefixes d(e)- and [ʔ]e-. Since MacKenzie’s (1961) assertion that “[e] must be taken as a separate entity and not as a form of d[e]-,” it has been taken for granted that these are etymologically unrelated forms. This assertion has been repeated in the context of studies of similar phenomena (e.g. Paul, 2003, 70). I assert that these formatives and all other imperfective marking strategies in Kurdish varieties actually have a unary source, a locative circumposition preserved in some varieties.

Locatives are one of several possible origins of an optional periphrastic progressive (Bybee et al.,1994). Through the imperfective cycle, an innovative, progressive construction becomes categorical--it must be used when there is an act-in-progress reading; subsequently, the progressive construction becomes a generalized imperfective. Thereare several locative constructions in Kurdish varieties, including the Mukriyanî locative circumposition de NP=da, which I have identified as a possible source for the Kurdish imperfective markers. Imperfective prefixes surface variably as d-, de-, [ʔ]e-, =y e-, =e me- and Ø- in Northern, Central, and Southern Kurdish varieties. Additionally, in SouthernKurdish varieties, there are imperfective suﬀixes -a,-ya, and-ẅa. By combining the forms of the imperfective prefixes and suﬀixes attested in Southern Kurdish varieties (Fattah,2000) with those found in Northern and Central Kurdish (Matras et al., 2016), a compelling account unifying these forms as the reflexes of an original periphrastic progressive built upon the locative circumposition takes shape.

The Kurdish varieties that tend to loose /d/s and the varieties that have the morpheme [ʔ]e- are not equal sets; this fact convinced MacKenzie (1961) that the prefixes d(e)- and [ʔ]e- are unrelated. Many regional languages across language families andsub-families lose /d/s. According to McCarus (2009), the Zagros-d phenomenon is the postvocalic realization of d, softened to glide like -i-, or -w-, or contracting with an adjacent high vowel. However, this is an over-simplification. The phenomenon, as McCarus (2009) describes it, is true diachronically for all Kurdish varieties, but it is now limited to the Zagros region as a synchronic phonological phenomenon. Additionally, insome varieties Zagros-d occurs after sonorants. In others, Zagros-d occurs across wordboundaries (sandhi), and the lenited variant was extended to inapplicable environments. This analogical extension is not the same as “Neo-Grammarian” Sound Change, whichis physiological and must occur in all applicable environments. This variability is whatcaused the discrepancy that ledMacKenzie(1961) to conclude that these are unrelatedmorphemes, which I reject here.

In this study, I examine data from the Manchester Kurdish dialect corpus (Matraset al., 2016) supplemented by Southern Kurdish data from (Fattah, 2000). Based on these data, I show that only regular well-documented sound changes give rise to all of the diversity observed in Kurdish varieties. Both the imperfective prefixes and suﬀixes arethe remnants of a circumpositional construction, which has not survived in all varieties. Even varieties that appear to have no imperfective marking have preserved formatives that indicate having developed a marker from the locative circumposition and losing it due to regular sound change. Furthermore, the sandhi forms extended to inapplicable environments resulting in the prefix e- are found within each of the subgroupings developed based on verbal formatives, which is to be expected of analogical phenomena.
