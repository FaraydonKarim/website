Karim, Shuan O. forthcoming. Akkadian [e]. To appear in the Journal of the American Oriental Society.

Abstract

There are several features of Akkadian which set it apart from other Semitic languages. One such feature is the assumption of seven `Alef’s which descended from Proto-Semitic phonemes, *ʔ, *h, *ʕ, *ɣ, *ħ, and sometimes *w and *j. The standard account is that they merged before being lost in nearly every environment. Additionally, ʔ3-5 interact with the low-back vowel, [a], producing [e]. There have been several descriptions of and explanations of these facts (e.g. Tropper (1995), Kogan (2001, 2002, 2003), Huehnergard (2003)). Most recently, Huehnergard (2013, 2014) has produced a convincing explanation of how [e] has disseminated throughout roots analogically corrupting ``root integrity.'' The reinterpretation of sandhi variants to all contexts and in many cases to all lexemes associated with a particular root proves to be a neat solution.

Another issue regarding the shift from [a] to [e] inadequately understood is in which environments [e] surfaces to be reanalyzed. First of all, the former presence of a guttural consonant (*ʕ, *ɣ or *ħ), is unlikely to cause [a] to be raised and fronted to [e] from an articulatory standpoint. Simply put, the backward and downward movement of the tongue required to produce a guttural consonant runs counter to the movement required to produce the vowel [e]. There must have been a medial stage where the Proto-Semitic gutturals took a form capable of causing raising to [e]. This theory has previously been proposed (Kouwenberg, 2006). Although, Kouwenburg’s explanation has been ignored to some degree because it does not adequately address the fact that the guttural *ħ was most often not lost at all. Another problem is that there are several roots that have [e] and no apparent conditioning environment.

I provide a solution to these issues clarifying the conditioning environments obscured by Akkadian orthography and Semitic derivational morphology. I show that voicing assimilation alone is responsible for the different outcomes of *ħ and *ʕ which have the same distribution. This assimilation has parallels in non-guttural roots such as *√ntn which becomes √ndn in Akkadian. Whether *ʕ and *ħ surface as <∅> or <ḫ> depends less on the underlying form than on assimilation to the voicing value of the adjacent consonant radical. With this analysis, there is no reason left to reject Kowenberg’s (2006) theory that gutturals became glides which cause raising to [e]. This is with the slight amendment that it is the voiced variant *ʕ alone which becomes a glide

Karim, Shuan O. forthcoming. The Ezafe and the Article. To appear in Advances in IranianLinguistics II.

Abstract

There are a variety of different nominal systems within the Western Iranian languages. Nominals in some of these languages are maximally sensitive to case, number, gender, animacy, ezafe (modification), and definiteness. However, most of these languages only inflect for a subset of these categories. An additional peculiarity of these systems is that some of these languages feature wildly differing systems when inflectional features interact. For instance, Zazaki features a caseless system on indefinite nouns, a bicasual (two case) system on definite nouns, and as many as four cases \citep[following][]{Paul1998a} on modified nouns. This paper explores, synchronically and diachronically, the nominal systems observed in Western Iranian languages focusing on the interaction between (in)definiteness and modification by attributive adjectives. This constitutes a near-comprehensive juxtaposition of data that can serve as an essential reference for anyone who seeks to study and understand the ezafe and the article in New Western Iranian.

Elsner, Micha and Andrea D Sims, et al. 2019. Modeling morphological learning, typology, and change: What can the neural sequence-to-sequence framework contribute? Journal of Language Modelling 7.1, 126-170.

Abstract

We survey research using neural sequence-to-sequence models as computational models of morphological learning and learnability. We discuss their use in determining the predictability of inflectional exponents, in making predictions about language acquisition and in modeling language change. Finally, we make some proposals for future work in these areas.
